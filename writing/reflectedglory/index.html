This is a follow up on Identity - or Keep Your Identity Small by Paul Graham.

I’ve added the relevant excerpts below in case you have not read that already, though I highly recommend that you do.

The essay is little more than 10 years old at this point. I’m disappointed because no one has written a good method on how to act on the advice which Paul shares in that piece.

What do you mean by identity?

Identity, in the crudest sense are the words (and ideas) that you use to describe yourself - in your head.

For instance, while you might be someone who build rocket ships, your identity might be that of simply someone who likes to play with explosives.

Or “I am a voyager making ships for space travel”

Here is a short mental exercise for yourself. Fill each of these blanks with at least 3 small phrases:

  1. I am _____
  2. I can do _____
  3. It is my duty to _____

Here are some of what I came up with when I first did this exercise a few year ago:

  1. I am {Gujarati, guy, techie}
  2. I can do {machine learning, writing, meditation}
  3. I love my {parents, country and people I’ve worked with}

The labels in {} are (in the very crude sense) - my identity. Notice that these labels do not always have to be professional, personal or even Truth in the strictest sense. They don’t have to align with reality in any meaningful way. They simply describe how you perceive yourself.

These innocous labels have been an incredible mental handicap.

Paul Graham stated how keeping your identity can be massive advantage:

The most intriguing thing about this theory, if it’s right, is that it explains not merely which kinds of discussions to avoid, but how to have better ideas. If people can’t think clearly about anything that has become part of their identity, then all other things being equal, the best plan is to let as few things into your identity as possible.
